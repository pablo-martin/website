Exploring Medicare Data

I found an interesting dataset gathered by data for democracy on Medicare drug expenditure. It includes data on class B (clinics or hospitals) and class D (prescription) drugs that went thru Medicare between 2011 and 2015. It also includes Pharma lobbying information for this period. Given the current opioid epidemic, I wanted to explore these data to gain some insight into the economics of Medicare drug expenditure.

First, I'll plot how 14 different opiate medications were administed in hospitals or clinics. Below is the total expenditure between 2011 and 2015:



There was a sharp increase in Morphine administration for 2015, and a steady decline for Fentanyl and Hydromorphone. Now let's take a look at the much larger prescription opiate figures:



Total expenditure stayed constant except for Oxycodone and Morphine, both of which had a sharp increase in 2014. If we also look at the # of people using the drug, we can see that for Oxycodone, the increase is not due to an increase in price, but rather an increase in beneficiaries. There is also a linear increase of users for Tramadol:



Finally, let's look at how Big Pharma lobbying contributions looked like between 2011 and 2015. We will restrict our search to pharmaceutical companies that produced opiates.



Overall, there is a noticeable increase in expenditure for prescription drugs (Oxycodone and Morphine) and number of users (Oxycodone and Tramadol). 2014 saw the biggest increase in opiate use, however, the increasing trend for Oxycodone in particular seems to have started since at least 2011. Furthermore, lobbying contributions stayed at a constant level, suggesting little impact on the increase in users and expenditure.
